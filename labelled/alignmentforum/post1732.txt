Â Or read this part first and then vote :) Boundaries, defined Boundaries include things like a cell membrane, a fence around yard, and a national border; see Part 1 . Â In short, a boundary is going to be something that separates the inside of a living system from the outside of the system. More fundamentally, a living system or organism will be defined as a) a part of the world, with b) a subsystem called its boundary which approximately causally separates another subsystem called its viscera from the rest of the world, where c) the boundary state decomposes into active and passive features that direct causal influence outward and inward respectively, such that d) the boundary and viscera together implement a decision-making process that perpetuates these four defining properties. One reason this combination of properties is interesting is that systems that make decisions to self-perpetuate tend to last longer and therefore be correspondingly more prevalent in the world; i.e., "survival of the survivalists". But more importantly, this definition will be directly relevant both to x-risk and to individual humans. Â In particular, we want the living system called humanity to use its model of itself to perpetuate its own existence, and we want AI to be respectful of that and hopefully even help us out with it. Â It might seem like continuing our species is just an arbitrary subjective preference among many that humanity would espouse. Â However, I'll later argue that the preservation of boundaries is a special kind of preference that can play a special role in bargaining, due to having a (relatively) objective or intersubjectively verifiable meaning. Discussion Non-violent boundary-crossings Real-world living systems sometimes do funky things like opening up their boundaries for each other, or even merging. Â For instance, consider two paramecia named Alex and Bailey. Â Part of Alex's decision rule r A l e x involves deciding to open Alex's boundary in order to exchange DNA with Bailey. Â If Alex does this in a way that allows Bailey's decision rule r B a i l e y to continue operating and decide for Bailey to open up, then the exchange of DNA has not violated Bailey's decision rule. Â In other words, while there is a boundary crossing event, one could say it is not a violation Bailey's boundary, because it respected (proceded in accordance with) Bailey's decision rule. Respect for boundaries as non-arbitrary coordination norms Epistemic status: speculation, but I think there's a theorem here. In my current estimation, respect for boundaries as described above is more than a matter of Alex and Bailey respecting each other's "preferences" as paramecia. Â I hypothesize that, in the emergence of fairly arbitrary colonies of living systems, standard protocols for respecting boundaries tend to emerge as well. Â In other words, respect for boundaries may be a "Schelling" concept that plays a crucial role in coordinating and governing positive-sum interactions between living systems. Â Essentially, preferences that are easily expressed in broadly and intersubjectively meaningful concepts â€” like Shannon's mutual information and Pearl's causation â€” are more likely to be pluralistically represented and agreed upon than other more idiosyncratic preferences. Incidentally, respect for human autonomy â€” the ability to make decisions â€” is something that many humans want to preserve through the advent of pervasive AI services and/or super-human agents. Â Interestingly, respect for autonomy is one of the most strongly codified ethical principles for how the scientific establishment â€” a kind of super-human intelligence â€” is supposed to treat experimental human subjects. Â See the Belmont Report , which is not only required reading for scientists performing human studies at many US universities, but also carries legal force in defining violations of human rights by the scientific establishment. Functional decision theory (FDT) FDT ( Yudkowsky and Soares, 2017 ) presents a promising way for artificial or living systems in the physical world to coordinate better, by noticing that they're essentially implementing the same function and choosing their outputs accordingly. Â When an agent in the 3D world starts thinking like an FDT agent, it draws its boundary around all parts of world that are running the same function, and considers them all to be "itself". Â This raises a question: how do two identical or nearly identical algorithms recognize â€” or decide â€” that they are in fact implementing essentially the same function? Â I'm not going to go deep into that here, but my best short answer is that algorithms still need to draw some boundaries in some abstract algorithm space â€” e.g., in the Solomonoff prior or the speed prior â€” that delineate what are considered their inputs, their outputs, their internals, and their externals. Â So, FDT sort of punts the problem of where to draw boundaries, moving the question out of physical space and into the space of (possible) algorithms. Recap In this post, I delineated 8 problems that I intend to address in terms of a formal definitions of boundaries, and laid our the basic structure of the formal definition. Â A living system is defined in terms of a decomposition of the world (with state variable W ) into an environment (state: E ), active boundary (state: A ), passive boundary (state: P), and viscera (state: V). Â The boundary state B=(A,P) forms an "approximate directed Markov blanket" separating the viscera from the environment, with A mediating outward causal influence and P mediating inward causal influence. Â This allows conceiving of the living system (V,A,P) as engaged in decision making according to some decision rule r Î¸ : V Ã— A Ã— P â†’ Î” ( V Ã— A ) that approximates reality.
